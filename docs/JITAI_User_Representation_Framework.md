# JITAI User Representation Framework — Hypertension Medication Adherence

## Overview

This document defines the user representation framework for a Just-in-Time Adaptive Intervention (JITAI) system designed to support hypertension medication adherence. The system delivers personalized messages generated by a large language model (LLM) based on each user's profile and current context.

| Phase | Trigger | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Onboarding** (Trait Profile) | One-time | Builds a stable user profile: routine, barriers, beliefs, support |
| **Daily Check-In** (State Vector) | Every day | Captures what is happening right now: adherence, routine status, mood |
| **Periodic Update** (Profile Refresh) | Every 2 weeks or event-triggered | Refreshes traits that may have drifted |

---

## Part 1: User Representation

The user representation is a structured profile maintained for each participant. It has two layers: a **trait profile** (stable characteristics) and a **state vector** (dynamic, real-time signals). Together they form the LLM prompt context at every decision point.

### 1.1 Trait Profile (Stable)

Collected primarily during onboarding and updated periodically. They represent who the person is — their context, beliefs, and tendencies.

#### Medication Profile

| Attribute | What it captures |
|---|---|
| What medication(s) they take | Name and type of antihypertensive |
| Dosing frequency | How many times per day |
| Timing | When during the day each dose is taken |
| Memory aids | Pill organizer, phone alarm, or nothing |
| Physical difficulty | Trouble swallowing or opening packaging |

#### Daily Routine

| Attribute | What it captures |
|---|---|
| Weekday routine | Morning-to-midday activities, especially around medication time |
| Weekend routine | Whether it differs from weekday (disruption risk) |
| Medication anchor | What activity they link their medication to (e.g. breakfast) |
| Storage location | Where pills are kept at home |
| Schedule type | Fixed hours, shift work, or irregular (affects habit stability) |

#### Barriers (COM-B Profile)

The LLM classifies open-ended barrier responses into three domains. Users are not shown these categories — the AI does the binning behind the scenes.

| COM-B Domain | Sub-type | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Capability | Psychological | Forgetting, confusion about regimen, lack of knowledge |
| Capability | Physical | Trouble swallowing, can't open packaging |
| Opportunity | Physical | Not home, ran out of pills, can't afford refill |
| Opportunity | Social | No one to remind them, unsupportive environment |
| Motivation | Reflective | Doesn't feel necessary, concerns about side effects |
| Motivation | Automatic | Low habit strength, routine disruption |

#### Beliefs About Medication

| Attribute | What it captures |
|---|---|
| Necessity belief | How essential they feel the medication is for their health |
| Concerns belief | How worried they are about long-term effects or dependency |
| Illness understanding | Whether they understand why BP needs treatment even when asymptomatic |

#### Social Support & Healthcare Access

| Attribute | What it captures |
|---|---|
| Social support | Whether someone helps with reminders or prescription pickup |
| Refill access | How easy it is to get medication when needed |
| Healthcare engagement | When they last saw their doctor about BP |

#### Preferences

| Attribute | What it captures |
|---|---|
| Preferred name | What they'd like to be called in messages |
| Message frequency | How often they want to receive messages |
| Check-in timing | Preferred time of day for daily check-ins |
| Message tone | Short and direct, encouraging, or practical tips |

### 1.2 State Vector (Dynamic)

Captured daily via check-in. These are momentary signals that tell the system what is happening right now. Combined with the trait profile, they determine whether to send a message and what it should say.

| Variable | Captured | Feeds into |
|---|---|---|
| Adherence status | Daily | Core decision: did they take it today? |
| Active barrier | Daily (if missed) | Message content — what specifically to address today |
| Routine status | Daily | Disruption detection — is today a normal day? |
| Mood / stress level | Daily | Message tone — empathetic vs. practical vs. brief |
| Weekly self-efficacy | Weekly pulse | Motivational drift detection over time |
| Refill status | Periodic / triggered | Proactive supply management messaging |

## Part 2: Onboarding

One-time interaction that builds the trait profile. Structured into two sections: routine first, then self-perceived barriers. All questions are plain and direct — no clinical framing.

**Goal:** Collect the stable user profile in under 5 minutes. Section 1 covers routine. Section 2 covers barriers. The LLM classifies open-ended responses into variable bins — users are not shown categories.

### Section 1 — Routine

#### Block 1: Identity & Preferences

**Q1** — What would you like to be called?
- *Note:* Used for message personalization. No clinical significance.

**Q2** — When do you usually take your blood pressure medication?
- Options: Morning · Afternoon · Evening · It varies
- *Note:* Sets the primary daily decision point — when the system evaluates whether to check in.

**Q3** — How often do you want me to check in with you?
- Options: Every day · A few times a week · Once a week
- *Note:* Sets message frequency preference.

#### Block 2: Weekday Routine

**Q4** — Walk me through a typical weekday morning — what do you do from when you wake up until midday?
- *Note:* Open-ended. The LLM extracts the medication anchor point, routine structure, and cue availability from this response.

**Q5** — What do you usually pair your medication with — something you do right before or after taking it?
- Options: Breakfast · Morning coffee · Brushing teeth · Bedtime routine · Nothing specific
- *Note:* Identifies the environmental cue anchor. Used to detect when routine disruption might cause a miss.

**Q6** — Where do you keep your medication at home?
- Options: Kitchen · Bedroom · Bathroom · I carry it with me
- *Note:* Physical opportunity factor — whether the pill is accessible in the user's natural environment.

**Q7** — Do you use anything to help you remember?
- Options: Phone alarm · Pill organizer · A family member or friend reminds me · Nothing — I try to remember on my own
- *Note:* Memory aid profile. "Nothing" flag indicates a higher forgetting risk.

#### Block 3: Weekend & Schedule Type

**Q8** — Is your weekend routine different from your weekday routine?
- Options: No, it's the same · A little different · Yes, quite different
- *Note:* Routine disruption risk on weekends. Triggers separate weekend anchors if different.

**Q9** — How would you describe your weekly schedule overall?
- Options: Pretty consistent · Varies a bit · Very irregular (shift work, travel, etc.)
- *Note:* "Very irregular" flags high routine disruption risk across all days — shapes message timing and content strategy.

### Section 2 — Self-Perceived Barriers

Start from yesterday to make the question concrete and less abstract. Then ask about barriers in general. The LLM classifies responses — no categories are shown to the user.

#### Block 4: Recent Adherence

**Q10** — Did you take your medication yesterday?
- Options: Yes · No
- *Note:* Baseline adherence snapshot. If "No", opens Q11.

**Q11** — What got in the way yesterday?
- *Note:* Open-ended. LLM classifies into COM-B bins: logistical, motivational, financial, forgetfulness, etc. Only asked if Q10 = No.

#### Block 5: General Barriers

**Q12** — In general, what makes it hardest for you to take your medication consistently? You can mention more than one thing.
- *Note:* Open-ended. Broader barrier profile beyond just yesterday. LLM classifies and builds the COM-B barrier profile for this user.

**Q13** — Does anyone in your household or close to you help you with your medication — reminders, picking up prescriptions, anything like that?
- Options: Yes · No · I'd like some help but don't have it
- *Note:* Social support factor. "I'd like help but don't have it" is a social opportunity barrier.

#### Block 6: Beliefs & Knowledge

**Q14** — How do you feel about your blood pressure medication overall?
- Options: It's important — I take it seriously · I take it but have some doubts · Honestly, I'm not sure I need it
- *Note:* Necessity belief. Strongest single predictor of long-term adherence. Shapes motivational framing of all future messages.

**Q15** — Do you ever worry about taking it long term — like side effects or getting dependent on it?
- Options: Not really · A little · Yes, quite a bit
- *Note:* Concerns belief. Combined with Q14, gives the necessity–concerns differential — the most predictive attitudinal profile.

**Q16** — Did you know that high blood pressure usually has no symptoms — you can feel completely fine and still be at risk?
- Options: Yes, I knew that · I'd heard something like that · No, I didn't know that
- *Note:* Attitudinal knowledge check. Not clinical — framed as a fact, not a test. Shapes how educational future messages need to be.

## Part 3: Daily Check-In

Sent once per day at the user's preferred medication time. The goal is to capture the state vector in under 90 seconds — three to four items maximum. Button responses wherever possible. Open-ended only when the user has missed their dose and a barrier needs to be identified.

### Standard Flow

**Q1** — Did you take your medication today?
- Options: Yes, done · Not yet · Missed it
- *Note:* Core proximal outcome. This single question drives the branching logic for the entire check-in.
  - IF "Yes, done" → proceed to Q2 (routine/mood)
  - IF "Not yet" → proceed to Q3 (brief nudge, follow up in 2 hours)
  - IF "Missed it" → proceed to Q4 (barrier probe)

### Branch A — If "Yes"

**Q2** — How is your day going?
- Options: Normal day · A bit out of routine · Stressful or difficult day
- *Note:* Routine and mood status. "Normal day + good mood" = brief reinforcement or nothing. "Disrupted or stressful" = short supportive message. This prevents notification fatigue for adherent users.

### Branch B — If "Not yet"

**Q3** — Is anything stopping you right now, or just haven't gotten to it yet?
- Options: Just haven't gotten to it yet · Something came up
- *Note:* Light touch — avoids pressure. The system follows up in 2 hours if no adherence is confirmed.

### Branch C — If "Missed it"

**Q4** — What got in the way today?
- Options: Forgot · Too busy · Wasn't home · Side effects · Didn't feel like it · Ran out of medication
- *Note:* Active barrier for today. LLM uses this to select the content type for the outgoing message. Maps to COM-B: "Forgot/Too busy" = Capability; "Wasn't home/Ran out" = Opportunity; "Side effects/Didn't feel like it" = Motivation.

### Weekly Addition (once per week, replaces mood question)

**Q5** — How confident are you feeling about your medication this coming week?
- Options: Very confident · Somewhat confident · Not very confident
- *Note:* Weekly self-efficacy pulse. Two consecutive drops trigger a periodic profile update.

## Part 4: Periodic Interactions

These interactions refresh parts of the trait profile that may have changed. They are not daily — they run on a schedule or when triggered by signals from the daily check-in. Mood is a periodic question, not a daily one. Periodic questions cover context like mood, barriers, and refill status rather than burdening the onboarding.

Periodic interactions fire on two conditions: (1) a fixed schedule (every 2 weeks / monthly), or (2) an event triggered from the daily check-in data. Always introduced with context so the user understands why they're being asked again.

### Scheduled — Every 2 Weeks

#### Barrier Refresh

Reassesses whether primary barriers have changed. The most predictive and most dynamic part of the profile.

**Q1** — Over the past couple of weeks, has anything made it harder to take your medication?
- Options: Yes, a few things · Not really, it's been fine
- *Note:* Detects barrier drift. If "Yes", opens Q2.

**Q2** — What's been getting in the way?
- *Note:* Open-ended. LLM re-classifies and updates the COM-B profile. Only asked if Q1 = Yes.

### Scheduled — Monthly

#### Routine Check

Checks whether daily routine has shifted — new job hours, travel, life changes. Routine is the most common source of adherence disruption.

**Q3** — Has your daily routine changed at all recently — new work hours, travel, anything like that?
- Options: No, same as usual · Yes, things have shifted a bit
- *Note:* If "Yes", triggers Q4 and prompts reminder time update.

**Q4** — What's changed?
- *Note:* Open-ended. LLM updates routine profile and may suggest updating the medication anchor or check-in time. Only asked if Q3 = Yes.

#### Mood & Context Check

Mood is a periodic question, not a daily one. Introduced gently to gauge relevance.

**Q5** — Sometimes things going on in life — stress, a rough week, bad weather — can affect how we feel about taking medication. Has anything like that been affecting you lately?
- Options: Not really · A little bit · Yes, it's been weighing on me
- *Note:* Situational context. If relevant patterns emerge across users, mood becomes a standard tailoring variable. If not, it is deprioritized.

**Q6** — What's been going on?
- *Note:* Open-ended. Feeds situational context into the LLM prompt for upcoming messages. Only asked if Q5 = "A little bit" or "Yes".

### Event-Triggered

These fire automatically based on signals from the daily check-in data. No fixed schedule — triggered by behavioral patterns.

| Trigger Condition | What Fires | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 3+ missed doses in 7 days | Barrier reassessment: "This week seems tough — what's been going on?" | Detects sustained barrier, updates COM-B profile |
| Side effect reported in daily check-in | "You mentioned side effects — has this been happening a lot? Worth mentioning to your doctor." | Updates concerns belief, avoids clinical advice |
| Routine disrupted 3+ consecutive days | "Looks like your routine has shifted — would you like to update your reminder time?" | Updates routine anchor, prevents future misses |
| Self-efficacy drops 2 weeks in a row | Re-engagement message + open question about what's changed | Detects motivational drift, triggers profile review |
| Refill window approaching | "Are you running low on your medication?" | Proactive supply management |
| User replies "Update my info" | Menu of profile sections to revisit | User-initiated profile correction |

## Part 6: Key Design Decisions

These decisions emerged from team discussion and are recorded here for reference.

| Decision | Rationale |
|---|---|
| AI classifies barriers, not the user | Prompting the LLM to extract variable bins from open-ended responses is more natural and avoids forcing users into rigid categories they may not identify with |
| Routine before barriers in onboarding | Asking about routine first is less confrontational and gives the LLM context to make barrier responses more meaningful |
| No side effect focus in daily check-in | Too much focus on side effects risks medicalizing the interaction. Side effects appear only as one barrier option, not a standalone question |
| Demographics minimal | Demographics are only useful for message wording (e.g. what to call the user). Not a primary design variable |
| Mood is periodic, not daily | If no one reports mood as a factor, it should not be used. Periodic introduction allows the team to evaluate its relevance before committing |
| Simulate first, then real patients | Role-play scenarios with synthetic data to test the system before involving real participants. If it cannot handle simulated responses, it is not ready for real people |
| "Did you take your medicine?" is the core signal | Start simple. Everything else builds on whether they took it or not. Consider both trait (anticipatable patterns) and state (momentary disruptions) |
